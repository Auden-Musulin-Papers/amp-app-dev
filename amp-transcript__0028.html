0001      Since then the Auden industry has not been idle.  An
0002 excellent biography was published1 and kin this the Austrian
0003 period was dealt with but not locally researched.  Edward
0004 Mendelson edited Auden´s early works and wrote superb commentaries.
0005 There has been a biography of Chester Kallman.  Writers of PhD
0006 theses have been out here and have shaken us up, particularly
0007 Michael O´Sullivan of Trinity College, Dublin who - not speaking
0008 a word of German - organised a full-scale exhibition and an
0009 international symposium in Vienna.  The editor of this volume
0010 Peter Müller had brought Michael out to see me at Friedau in the
0011 preceding year;  now he came again, and after the closure,
0012 exhausted, he spent a long weekend in the country to recover.
0013 We dug out ancient files containing original Auden manuscripts,
0014 letters, personal notes and newspaper cuttings, and now we tried
0015 to winnow the wheat from the chaff.  After some general discussion
0016 it seemed to us all that there remain certain aspects of Auden´s
0017 life in Lower Austria which are not on record, or where they are,
0018 not from the worm´s eye view.  As an older man he was happier
0019 here than anywhere else:  he felt at home.  He was at Kirchstetten
0020 not only during the summer, ias is often said, but with
0021 interruptions for five months, depending on his engagements.
0022 In the course of these years he was still highly productive
0023 creative.  So that it might, we thought, be of value to put some
0024 of those things on record which would otherwise be lost.  Not
0025 for the first time self.-criticism was expressed, but while doubts
0026 concerning self-importancesand sell-out of firiendship were not
0027 entirely banished, the view prevailed that the local witness
0028 needs to be put on paper.  Scholarship is at work elsewhere;
0029 here a few appendices and footnotes are on offer.
0030      Auden´s opinions on biographies of creative artists in general
0031 were, as Humphrey Carpenter pointed out, highly contradictory.
0032 Again and again he said that the private life of poets and other
0033 people engaged in creative work is none of the public´s business
0034 but he also said:´`The biography of an artist, if his life as a whole
0035 was sufficiently interesting, is permissible, provided that the
0036 biographer and his readers realise that such an account throws no
0037 light whatsoever upon the artist´s work."  And "I do believe,
0038 however, that, more often than most people realise, khis works
0039 may throw light upon his life."
